Over the past half century, the math community underwent a colossal effort to classify all the finite simple groups. A lot of these groups followed some structure, but there were 26 that defied all that and seemed to appear in a sporadic fashion, so the sporadic simple groups they were.

The finite portion of finite simple group is easy to understand. A simple group is one whose only normal subgroups are itself and the trivial group.

Alright, we are finally in territory I am almost familiar with. Group theory is based around a notion of groups, which are sets of items together with a group operation (like multiplication, for instance) that have certain properties. For instance, two items from the group composed with the group operation must result in another item of the group.

So in the context of simple groups I can now define some things in terms I understand. A subgroup is a subset of a group that preserves the group operation, that is when two items from the subgroup are composed with the group operation the result is from that subgroup.

The trivial group is a group whose only member is the identity. This is easy to understand I think (relative to the rest of this mess), as there are plenty of examples. 0 is the identity of a group with addition as its operation, 1 for multiplication, and so on.
